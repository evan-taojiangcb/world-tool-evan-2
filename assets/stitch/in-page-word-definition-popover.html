The Psychology of Delay: Why do we put things off?

Sarah Jenkins

Behavioral Scientist

It is a common human experience to delay tasks despite knowing there will be negative consequences. We often find ourselves cleaning the entire house when we should be working on a presentation, or scrolling through social media when a deadline looms.

This phenomenon is widely known as procrastination. While often mistaken for laziness, it is actually an emotional regulation problem, not a time management problem.

procrastination

/prəˌkræs.tɪˈneɪ.ʃən/
noun

The action of delaying or postponing something.

"He hated his own procrastination."

他讨厌自己的拖延。

Researchers suggest that we delay action because we feel unable to manage the negative moods around a task. We prioritize short-term mood repair over long-term goals.

lightbulb Did you know?

Chronic procrastination affects approximately 20% of the adult population, causing stress and reduced well-being.

To overcome this, psychologists recommend breaking large tasks into smaller, manageable chunks. This technique, often referred to as "chunking," reduces the psychological barrier to entry.
